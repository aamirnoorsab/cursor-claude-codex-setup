\## Post 1 — Machine Media: The Death of the Open Web



Mike King's original thesis coining "Machine Media" — the argument that the open web is dying as agent traffic overtakes human traffic, and that brands must treat owned content as a machine-addressable data layer, not a destination.



Key data points (his citations): AI bot traffic on Cloudflare +187% in 2025 vs. human +3.1%; agentic AI traffic +7,851% YoY; ClaudeBot crawls \~24,000 pages per referral; global publisher Google traffic -33% in 2025; small publishers lost 60-90% of referral traffic.



Core concepts for content production:

\- "Relevance Engineering" — the discipline of engineering brand, content, and data to be chosen, cited, and transacted on by machines. Positioned as a C-suite discipline, not an SEO-team task.

\- Six strategic moves: treat owned content as a data layer (API-first, MCP-ready); adopt agent protocols early (MCP, A2A, llms.txt, structured feeds); feed machines everything except your competitive moat; measure presence and transaction, not traffic; engineer brand to be agent-legible; reorganize around Relevance Engineering.

\## Post 2 — Beyond RAG: Why Every AI Search Platform Is Now Agentic



Deep technical breakdown of how AI search moved from single-shot RAG to agentic RAG, mapped to five Google patents, with a working GitHub audit tool.



Core concepts for content production — the six shifts agentic RAG forces:

1\. Win across many sub-retrievals (5-20 sub-queries per prompt), not one ranking.

2\. Atomic, scoped passages that survive pairwise comparison beat monolithic articles.

3\. Bridge entities determine multi-hop inclusion (get cited in answers where the user never typed your brand).

4\. Reflection cycles reward source diversity and contradiction-handling; one-sided salesy content gets filtered.

5\. Tool-callable content (calculators, APIs, structured endpoints) is a new content type — routers call tools instead of citing prose.

6\. Freshness is a critic-stage gate (dateModified schema, version numbers, "as of \[date]" framing).



New measurement layer he proposes: sub-query coverage, retrieval-to-citation ratio, reflection survival rate, bridge-entity centrality, tool-call inclusion, distillation stage-failure rate.

\## Post 3 — Moving from a Google-shaped Web to an Agent-shaped Web (Chunking)



Mike King's rebuttal of Google's (Danny Sullivan's) claim that "chunking" content for AI is a pointless manipulation tactic. Argues that structuring content into atomic, self-contained passages is both good UX and mechanically necessary for retrieval — backed by his own tool (BubbaChunk) showing measured cosine-similarity improvements from restructuring.



Core argument for content production:

\- Chunking (structuring content so passages perform in RAG retrieval) and writing for humans are not mutually exclusive.

\- Relevance is computed at the passage level, not the page level — each chunk is a discrete relevance object.

\- The web is shifting from "Google-shaped" (conform to one ranking system) to "agent-shaped" (content must be legible and recombinable by many agents). Structure becomes more important as context windows grow, not less (cites Infini-attention, Ring Attention, MoR research).

\- "Programmatic Legibility" — designing content so each chunk stands alone with clear entity, context, and claim.

\- Cites Dan Petrovic's research on grounding-chunk length (cross-references another expert in this source list).

\## Post 4 — "It's Just SEO" Rebuttal



Mike King's point-by-point rebuttal of the "GEO is just SEO" argument. Frames it as a business-maturity issue: dismissing AI search as "just SEO" signals to C-level decision-makers that you don't understand how business disciplines are structured.



Key arguments:

\- Different disciplines can share tactics but differ in value exchange — PR, link building, fundraising, and outbound sales all use the same mechanics (list, outreach, follow-up) but are four different teams and budget lines. AI search vs. SEO is the same distinction.

\- Legacy SEO tools give insights based on outdated retrieval paradigms; doing AI search well requires more than SEO (engineering-adjacent skills).

\- Data cited: ZipTie (top-10 ranking = \~25% chance of being an AI source); Profound (19% overlap between Google Search and ChatGPT visibility); Josh Blyskal (relevance matters more than position). Argument: optimizing the same way for retrieval yields \~20% hit rate — not enough.



Note: This post is the direct counterpoint to Lily Ray's "it's mostly just SEO" position (see youtube-transcripts/lilyray\_geo\_aeo\_llmo\_mozcon.txt). The King–Ray disagreement is a key tension in this research base.
